Mental Health — By the numbers

Over 1 in 5 U.S. adults (59.3 million) live with a mental illness, with nearly 1 in 20 experiencing serious mental illness. These conditions cause significant impact — including over 49,000 suicide deaths annually, roughly 50 million people experiencing behavioral health issues, and high barriers to care that leave roughly 25% of adults with mental illness untreated.

Make sure your friends aren't one of them.

{% if category|lower == 'anxiety' %}

Anxiety

Persistent worry and fear that feels hard to switch off

What it is

Anxiety is more than everyday stress — it's a persistent, excessive worry that's hard to control. It can show up as constant fear, racing thoughts, physical tension, avoiding situations, and catastrophic thinking even when there's no real threat. It's one of the most common mental health conditions and very treatable.

What you can do for them

  • Listen without dismissing — don't say "just relax" or "calm down"
  • Help them ground themselves with the 5 senses exercise
  • Breathe slowly together — it genuinely helps regulate the nervous system
  • Gently suggest talking to a doctor or therapist

{% elif category|lower == 'bipolar' %}

Bipolar Disorder

Extreme mood swings between emotional highs and deep lows

What it is

Bipolar disorder causes dramatic shifts in mood, energy, and activity — from manic episodes (elevated mood, reduced sleep, impulsive decisions) to depressive episodes (hopelessness, fatigue, withdrawal). These aren't just mood swings; they're intense, lasting episodes that significantly impact daily life.

What you can do for them

  • Stay consistent — routine and stability are genuinely helpful
  • Don't take manic behavior personally; recognize it as a symptom
  • Gently flag concerning behavior without confrontation
  • Encourage them to stay connected with their treatment plan

Resources

DBSA — Depression & Bipolar Support Alliance NAMI — Bipolar Disorder Guide NIMH — Bipolar Overview
{% elif category|lower == 'depression' %}

Depression

More than sadness — a persistent heaviness that dims everything

What it is

Depression isn't just feeling sad — it's a persistent low mood, loss of interest in things once enjoyed, low energy, trouble concentrating, and often feelings of worthlessness or hopelessness. It can make even small tasks feel impossible and affects how a person thinks, feels, and functions every day.

What you can do for them

  • Check in regularly — isolation makes depression worse
  • Offer small, low-pressure things to do together
  • Avoid telling them to "cheer up" or "think positive"
  • Help them take the first step toward professional support

Resources

NAMI — Depression Guide NIMH — Depression Facts 7 Cups — Free emotional support chat
{% elif category|lower == 'personality disorder' %}

Personality Disorder

Deep patterns in thinking and relating that cause ongoing distress

What it is

Personality disorders involve long-standing patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that differ significantly from cultural expectations and cause distress or problems in daily life. Types include borderline, narcissistic, and avoidant personality disorders, among others. They often stem from early experiences and are treatable with the right therapy.

What you can do for them

  • Stay calm and consistent — unpredictability can worsen symptoms
  • Set clear, compassionate boundaries for yourself
  • Don't diagnose or label them — focus on how they feel
  • Encourage therapy, especially DBT which is highly effective

Resources

NAMI — Personality Disorder Guide NIMH — BPD Overview DBT Self Help — skills & resources
{% elif category|lower == 'stress' %}

Stress

An overloaded system struggling to keep up with demands

What it is

Stress is the body and mind's response to pressure or overwhelming demands. While some stress is normal, chronic or intense stress takes a real toll — on sleep, physical health, decision-making, and relationships. Left unaddressed, it can develop into anxiety or depression.

What you can do for them

  • Ask what's weighing on them — sometimes being heard is enough
  • Help them identify what's in and out of their control
  • Encourage breaks, rest, and movement where possible
  • Offer practical help if the stress is situation-based

Resources

APA — Stress & Coping Resources Headspace — Stress & mindfulness app Mental Health Foundation — Stress guide
{% elif category|lower == 'suicidal' %}

Suicidal Ideation

This is serious — please act now, not later

What it is

Suicidal ideation means having thoughts about ending one's life. It ranges from passive thoughts like "I wish I wasn't here" to active planning. Any expression of this should be taken seriously — never assume it's for attention. Asking directly about suicide does not put the idea in someone's head; it shows you care.

What you can do right now

  • Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" — it helps
  • Stay with them. Don't leave them alone if risk feels immediate
  • Listen without judgment — don't try to fix or argue
  • Call or text 988 together — it's free, confidential, 24/7

Resources — act now

988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988 Crisis Text Line — text HOME to 741741 AFSP — American Foundation for Suicide Prevention
